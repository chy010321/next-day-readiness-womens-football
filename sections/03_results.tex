\section{Results}
\subsection{Analytic sample}
The primary cohort contained 14,582 eligible player-day pairs from 50 players. Team A contributed 3,812 pairs in 2020 and 5,881 in 2021, whereas Team B contributed 2,406 and 2,483 pairs, respectively (Table~\ref{tab:sample}). Across the primary cohort, both current-day and next-day readiness had a median of 7.0 (interquartile range 6.0--8.0). The median daily load was 350 session-RPE units (interquartile range 12--600), and 75.0\% of eligible pairs occurred on days with non-zero training load.

The complete calendar panel contained 36,550 player-days. Of these, 16,997 (46.5\%) had observed next-day readiness and 14,631 (40.0\%) had both current- and next-day readiness with the required 7-day load history. The common complete-case comparison cohort retained 14,582 pairs (39.9\% of the calendar panel; Supplementary Tables~S4--S5 and S8). These counts are retrospective eligibility counts because they condition on an observed next-day outcome; they should not be interpreted as prospective deployment-availability rates. The primary analysis therefore estimates predictive accuracy among report-complete, outcome-observed player-days rather than coverage across all calendar days.

\subsection{Primary incremental-value comparison}
After separate full-pipeline tuning within source-team 2020 data, all comparators selected $\alpha=1000$ from the prespecified grid (Supplementary Table~S1). Under the primary 28-observation sequential residual window, \Pone{} did not improve the prespecified primary metric, MAE, over \Pzero{} in any evaluation setting (Table~\ref{tab:primary}; Figure~\ref{fig:incremental}). Within Team A, \Pzero{} and \Pone{} had MAEs of 0.717 and 0.724, respectively (\DeltaMAE{} +0.007, 95\% CI $-$0.005 to +0.023). Within Team B, MAE was 0.619 for \Pzero{} and 0.637 for \Pone{} (\DeltaMAE{} +0.018, $-$0.005 to +0.047). In cross-team evaluation, \DeltaMAE{} was +0.022 ($-$0.005 to +0.053) from Team A to Team B and +0.004 ($-$0.007 to +0.016) from Team B to Team A. All four intervals crossed zero. Because no smallest practically important MAE difference was prespecified, these findings are interpreted as no stable evidence of incremental MAE improvement, rather than as formal proof of practical equivalence.

\subsection{Sensitivity analyses and calibration}
Changing the sequential residual window from 14 to 56 earlier eligible forecasts did not reveal a stable MAE advantage for \Pone{} (Figure~\ref{fig:window}; Supplementary Table~S2). The reduced-monitoring model \Ptwo{} likewise did not demonstrate an incremental MAE advantage over \Pzero{} (Supplementary Table~S2).

The wellness-item-missingness sensitivity analysis used 14,631 player-day pairs with observed current and next-day readiness and 7-day load history, only 49 more pairs than the primary complete-case cohort. It used source-training median imputation and wellness-specific missingness indicators. Estimated \DeltaMAE{} values for the imputed full model were +0.008 ($-$0.004 to +0.025), +0.020 ($-$0.006 to +0.049), +0.024 ($-$0.003 to +0.059), and +0.004 ($-$0.006 to +0.016) across the four settings, respectively (Supplementary Figure~S2; Supplementary Table~S7). This analysis did not reveal stable incremental improvement, but its scope is restricted to item-level wellness missingness among player-days with observed current and next-day readiness. It does not address dates on which the daily questionnaire was not completed.

Linear calibration slopes ranged from 0.908 to 0.928 for \Pzero{} and from 0.918 to 0.920 for \Pone{} under the primary window (Supplementary Figure~S3; Supplementary Table~S3). Under the stated regression diagnostic of observed on predicted readiness, slopes below 1 indicate that the predictions varied modestly more than the observed scores. Player-level \Pone{}--\Pzero{} MAE differences varied within each setting (Supplementary Figure~S4); this descriptive heterogeneity does not identify stable player-specific benefit or causal effects.
