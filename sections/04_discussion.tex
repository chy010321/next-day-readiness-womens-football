\section{Discussion}
The principal finding was that the evaluated daily workload--wellness panel did not provide stable incremental forecasting value beyond a personalised autoregressive readiness baseline. Across two within-team chronological evaluations and two bidirectional cross-team transportability evaluations, the full monitoring model had equal or higher MAE than the comparator that used current readiness plus the same sequential personal residual adjustment. The result was directionally robust to alternative residual-history windows and to a reduced monitoring panel.

This finding should be interpreted narrowly. It does not show that training load, sleep, soreness, fatigue, stress, or mood are unimportant in athlete management. These measures may support communication, identify concerns not captured by a single score, aid clinical reasoning, or be associated with later performance and health outcomes. Rather, the present result concerns a specific incremental forecasting task: whether the tested panel improved next-day self-reported readiness once a model already had access to current readiness and the player's own earlier forecast errors. In that setting, the added variables did not show a consistent reduction in absolute error.

The result is compatible with several explanations. First, current readiness may already integrate a substantial amount of the information players use when reporting their short-term state, including recent workload and recovery experiences. Second, the individual residual adjustment may capture persistent player-specific reporting tendencies and unmeasured contextual factors. Third, the added variables may contain useful information for some players or situations but not enough stable, transportable information to improve average error across both teams. Finally, self-reported measures collected in the same monitoring system may share common measurement characteristics, so the apparent breadth of the panel does not necessarily translate into independent predictive signal.

The two-team evaluation is a particular strength. In athlete monitoring, random record-level splits can produce overly optimistic estimates when temporally and hierarchically related observations from the same player and team context occur in both training and testing data \citep{Bergmeir2018,Roberts2017}. The present design instead separated source and target periods and, in two scenarios, source and target teams. The lack of stable incremental gain for \Pone{} under these conditions suggests that monitoring models should be evaluated against strong personal-history baselines before additional variables are assumed to add practical value. The cross-team findings should nevertheless be regarded as a two-team transportability assessment, not as evidence of universal external validity.

The findings also provide a caution against equating a larger feature set with better monitoring. Session-RPE and wellness questionnaires are easy to collect, but routine monitoring has opportunity costs. A parsimonious readiness-first approach may be adequate for short-horizon forecasting in some settings, particularly when it is updated with the athlete's own recent outcomes. This does not support discontinuing broader monitoring; rather, it supports aligning measurement burden with a clearly specified decision problem. When the decision concerns next-day readiness forecasting, future studies should quantify whether each additional measure improves error, calibration, decision utility, or a distinct clinical or performance endpoint.

Several limitations require emphasis. First, readiness was a self-reported 1--10 measure, not an objective performance, clinical, or injury endpoint. Second, eligibility required complete current-day wellness reports, so conclusions apply to report-complete days. Missingness itself may be informative and deserves separate modelling. Third, the sample involved only two teams in one league and one monitoring protocol. Fourth, this analysis used subjective data only; objective GPS and contextual information might change the incremental-value comparison. Fifth, the models were deliberately parsimonious ridge-based implementations using a fixed regularisation setting. The study was designed to test incremental value relative to a strong baseline, not to conduct an exhaustive algorithm competition or an extensive hyperparameter search. Sixth, the online residual adjustment assumes that earlier target-period readiness scores are available, which is appropriate for ongoing monitoring but not for a true new-player cold-start setting. Finally, the study evaluated predictive performance rather than causal effects of workload or wellness; the results should therefore not be interpreted as estimates of the consequences of changing any individual monitoring variable \citep{Shmueli2010}.

Future work should test the same question in independent women's football cohorts, include objective external-load data and contextual features, evaluate missingness-aware models, and compare prediction performance with decision-analytic utility. Importantly, such studies should retain strict temporal alignment, participant-aware validation, calibration assessment, and explicit comparison with personal-history baselines \citep{Collins2024,Debray2023TRIPODCluster}.

\section{Conclusions}
In two elite women's football teams, a daily workload--wellness panel did not demonstrate stable incremental value beyond a personalised autoregressive baseline for next-day self-reported readiness. The results challenge the assumption that more routine monitoring variables necessarily improve short-term readiness forecasts. They do not establish that workload or wellness are causally irrelevant, nor do they justify removing these measures from broader athlete-management practice.

\section*{Practical applications}
\begin{itemize}
\item Evaluate daily monitoring panels against a strong player-specific readiness-history baseline rather than against a weak or randomly split benchmark.
\item Report calendar-time validation and calibration when the intended use is next-day forecasting.
\item Treat additional monitoring variables as valuable only when they improve a prespecified decision-relevant outcome, or when they serve a separate clinical or communication purpose.
\item Do not infer from this study that teams should stop collecting workload or wellness information; the result is specific to incremental prediction of next-day self-reported readiness.
\end{itemize}
