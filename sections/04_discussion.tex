\section{Discussion}
The principal finding was that the evaluated daily workload--wellness panel did not demonstrate stable incremental forecasting value beyond a personalised readiness-history baseline. Across two within-team chronological evaluations and two bidirectional cross-team transportability evaluations, the full monitoring model did not lower the prespecified primary metric, MAE, after both comparators had been tuned using the same complete online deployment pipeline. All player-cluster bootstrap intervals for the MAE difference crossed zero. The direction of the result was consistent across alternative residual-history windows, a reduced monitoring panel, and a wellness-item-missingness sensitivity analysis conditional on observed current- and next-day readiness. Because no smallest practically important MAE difference or decision threshold was specified, the findings do not establish formal equivalence or absence of all practical value.

This finding should be interpreted narrowly. It does not show that training load, sleep, soreness, fatigue, stress, or mood are unimportant in athlete management. These measures may support communication, identify concerns not captured by a single score, aid clinical reasoning, or be associated with later performance and health outcomes. Rather, the present result concerns a specific incremental forecasting task: whether the tested panel improved next-calendar-day self-reported readiness once a model already had access to current readiness and the player's own earlier forecast errors. In that setting, the added variables did not demonstrate stable reduction in absolute error.

The result is compatible with several explanations. First, current readiness may already integrate a substantial amount of the information players use when reporting their short-term state, including recent workload and recovery experiences. Second, the individual residual adjustment may capture persistent player-specific reporting tendencies and unmeasured contextual factors. Third, the added variables may contain useful information for some players or situations but not enough stable, transportable information to improve average error across both teams. Finally, self-reported measures collected in the same monitoring system may share common measurement characteristics, so the apparent breadth of the panel does not necessarily translate into independent predictive signal.

The two-team evaluation is a particular strength. In athlete monitoring, random record-level splits can produce overly optimistic estimates when temporally and hierarchically related observations from the same player and team context occur in both training and testing data \citep{Bergmeir2018,Roberts2017}. The present design instead separated source and target periods and, in two scenarios, source and target teams. The absence of stable incremental gain for \Pone{} under these conditions supports evaluating monitoring models against strong personal-history baselines before additional variables are assumed to add predictive value. The cross-team findings should nevertheless be regarded as a two-team transportability assessment under sequential target-team adaptation, not as zero-shot external validation or evidence of universal external validity. In particular, the player-cluster bootstrap intervals quantify uncertainty across athletes in each target team rather than across a broad population of teams.

The findings also provide a caution against equating a larger feature set with better short-horizon prediction. Session-RPE and wellness questionnaires are easy to collect, but routine monitoring has opportunity costs. The present results support aligning measurement burden with a clearly specified decision problem, not discontinuing broader monitoring. When the decision concerns next-calendar-day readiness forecasting, future studies should quantify whether each additional measure improves a prespecified error target, calibration, decision utility, or a distinct clinical or performance endpoint.

Several limitations require emphasis. First, readiness was a self-reported 1--10 measure, not an objective performance, clinical, or injury endpoint. Second, the primary paired comparison required complete current-day wellness reports and observed next-day readiness, so it applies to report-complete, outcome-observed days. The wellness-item-missingness sensitivity analysis expanded the cohort by only 49 player-days and did not address dates on which the daily questionnaire, including current readiness, was not completed. Third, the sample involved only two teams in one league and one monitoring protocol. Fourth, this analysis used subjective data only; objective GPS and contextual information might change the incremental-value comparison. Fifth, the models were deliberately parsimonious linear/ridge implementations. Weekday and month were entered as numeric terms rather than as one-hot or cyclic functions, so non-linear or cyclical calendar effects may not have been fully represented. Regularisation was tuned separately for each comparator using full-pipeline internal chronological validation, but the study was designed to test incremental value relative to a strong baseline rather than to conduct an exhaustive algorithm competition. Sixth, the online residual adjustment assumes that earlier target-period readiness scores are available, which is appropriate for ongoing monitoring but not for a true new-player cold-start setting. Finally, no smallest practically important MAE difference was prespecified, so the present analyses do not establish formal equivalence or decision utility. The study evaluated predictive performance rather than causal effects of workload or wellness; the results should therefore not be interpreted as estimates of the consequences of changing any individual monitoring variable \citep{Shmueli2010}.

Future work should test the same question in independent women's football cohorts, include objective external-load and contextual features, explicitly model days with absent questionnaires, prespecify decision-relevant performance thresholds, report cold-start performance before residual histories accrue, and compare prediction performance with decision-analytic utility. Such studies should retain strict temporal alignment, participant-aware validation, calibration assessment, and explicit comparison with personal-history baselines \citep{Collins2024,Debray2023TRIPODCluster}.

\section{Conclusions}
In two elite women's football teams, and for report-complete player-days evaluated with sequential personalisation, a daily workload--wellness panel did not demonstrate stable incremental MAE improvement beyond a personalised readiness-history baseline. The results challenge the assumption that more routine monitoring variables necessarily improve short-term readiness forecasts in this specific deployment setting. They do not establish that workload or wellness are causally irrelevant, nor do they justify removing these measures from broader athlete-management practice.

\section*{Practical applications}
\begin{itemize}
\item Evaluate daily monitoring panels against a strong player-specific readiness-history baseline rather than against a weak or randomly split benchmark.
\item Report calendar-time validation, calibration, the availability of deployable predictions, and the scope of uncertainty when the intended use is next-day forecasting.
\item Treat additional monitoring variables as having incremental predictive value only when they improve a prespecified decision-relevant outcome; they may still serve separate clinical or communication purposes.
\item Do not infer from this study that teams should stop collecting workload or wellness information; the result is specific to incremental prediction of next-calendar-day self-reported readiness on report-complete days.
\end{itemize}
