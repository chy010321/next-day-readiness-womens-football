\section{Introduction}
Daily athlete-monitoring systems commonly capture internal training load and self-reported wellness to support training planning and athlete communication \citep{Halson2014,Brink2010,Rago2020,Saw2016,Thorpe2015}. Training duration, rating of perceived exertion (RPE), session-RPE load, sleep, fatigue, soreness, mood, stress, and perceived readiness can be collected repeatedly in field settings. Repeated self-reporting, however, creates reporting and data-management burdens and frequently yields missing observations. Monitoring studies should therefore define the intended decision problem before assuming that a larger panel is necessarily more useful \citep{Saw2016,Midoglu2024}.

The practical question is not simply whether workload and wellness variables correlate with readiness. Several studies indicate that wellness responses can vary with training exposure and that dose--response relations may be nonlinear or context-dependent \citep{Campbell2020,Campbell2021}. For next-day forecasting, the relevant question is conditional incremental value: does a broader panel improve prediction relative to a comparator that already has access to the athlete's current readiness and recent personal prediction history? If the current readiness score already integrates much of the information that an athlete uses to describe short-term state, a larger panel may add little average forecast information even though its individual variables remain useful for communication, clinical judgement, or training planning.

This distinction is particularly important for individualised monitoring. Between-player differences in baseline scores, reporting behaviour, and physical responses can be substantial, and analyses of football performance have shown that monitoring should consider individual variability rather than rely solely on team averages \citep{OlivaLozano2021}. Evidence from women's football should also be interpreted in population-specific contexts, because physical match demands and psychological performance factors can vary across female players and competitive environments \citep{Baptista2022,Pettersen2022}. Longitudinal data that repeatedly capture subjective load and wellness are therefore valuable for evaluating a clearly specified prediction task. SoccerMon provides publicly available monitoring data from two elite women's football teams \citep{Midoglu2024,SoccerMonZenodo}.

Prior work using the SoccerMon data stream has shown that readiness trends can be modelled with time-series approaches \citep{Wiik2019,Kulakou2022}. Feasibility demonstrations and algorithm comparisons, however, do not establish whether a daily workload--wellness panel improves forecast performance beyond a strong personalised readiness-history baseline, nor whether any apparent improvement is stable when a model is transferred between teams. This distinction matters because the present study is designed to evaluate prediction rather than causal explanation \citep{Shmueli2010}.

Prediction studies intended for future use should preserve temporal ordering and account for correlated player- and team-level observations rather than rely on a random record-level split \citep{Bergmeir2018,Roberts2017,Debray2023TRIPODCluster}. Calibration is also relevant because apparently acceptable average error can coexist with systematic disagreement between predicted and observed values \citep{VanCalster2019,Collins2024}. Finally, evaluating a population model in a second team probes transportability, but any target-team updating must be described explicitly because it changes the deployment scenario \citep{Janssen2008}.

Accordingly, the primary aim of this study was to assess whether a daily workload--wellness panel added incremental value beyond a personalised readiness-history baseline for forecasting next-calendar-day self-reported readiness in elite women's football. The secondary aim was to examine whether this comparison was directionally stable in within-team temporal testing and bidirectional two-team transportability testing under identical sequential personalisation. We considered incremental value practically credible only if it was demonstrated consistently across these prespecified settings.
