\title{Incremental Value of Daily Workload and Wellness Monitoring Beyond a Personalised Readiness-History Baseline for Next-Calendar-Day Readiness in Elite Women's Football}

\begin{abstract}
\textbf{Purpose:} Daily workload and wellness monitoring are widely used in elite football, yet it remains uncertain whether a larger monitoring panel improves short-horizon readiness forecasts beyond current readiness and a player's own earlier forecast errors. We assessed the incremental forecasting value and two-team transportability of a daily workload--wellness panel for next-calendar-day self-reported readiness in elite women's football.

\textbf{Methods:} This secondary analysis used publicly available SoccerMon subjective-monitoring data from 50 players in two elite women's football teams. The primary cohort comprised 14,582 player-day pairs with complete current-day wellness information, observed next-day readiness, and the 7-day load history used by the primary model. Predictors measured no later than day $t$ forecast readiness at $t+1$. A current-readiness model (\Pzero) and full-monitoring model (\Pone) were each tuned in three expanding chronological source-team 2020 folds. Tuning evaluated the complete deployment pipeline, including the same reset sequential player-specific residual adjustment subsequently used in 2021 testing. Models were evaluated within teams and bidirectionally across teams. The primary estimand was \DeltaMAE{} = MAE(\Pone) $-$ MAE(\Pzero), with 95\% player-cluster bootstrap confidence intervals.

\textbf{Results:} The full monitoring model did not reduce mean absolute error in any evaluation setting. \DeltaMAE{} was +0.007 (95\% CI $-$0.005 to +0.023) within Team A, +0.018 ($-$0.005 to +0.047) within Team B, +0.022 ($-$0.005 to +0.053) from Team A to Team B, and +0.004 ($-$0.007 to +0.016) from Team B to Team A. Positive values indicate higher error for \Pone. Results were directionally consistent across residual-history windows, a reduced-panel comparison, and a missingness-aware sensitivity analysis using source-training median imputation plus wellness-missingness indicators.

\textbf{Conclusions:} In these two elite women's football teams, the evaluated daily workload--wellness panel did not demonstrate stable incremental MAE value beyond a personalised readiness-history baseline. This finding does not imply that workload or wellness are causally irrelevant; rather, it challenges the assumption that adding routine daily variables necessarily improves next-calendar-day self-reported readiness forecasts.
\end{abstract}

\textbf{Keywords:} athlete monitoring; women's football; readiness to train; training load; wellness; personalised forecasting; transportability
