\title{Does Daily Workload and Wellness Monitoring Add Incremental Value Beyond a Personalised Autoregressive Baseline for Next-Day Readiness? A Two-Team Temporal Study in Elite Women's Football}

\begin{abstract}
\textbf{Purpose:} Daily workload and wellness monitoring are widely used in elite football, yet it remains uncertain whether a larger monitoring panel improves short-term readiness forecasts beyond an athlete's own recent readiness history. We assessed the incremental forecasting value and two-team transportability of a daily workload--wellness panel for next-day self-reported readiness in elite women's football.

\textbf{Methods:} This secondary analysis used publicly available SoccerMon subjective-monitoring data from 50 players in two elite women's football teams. Eligible records comprised 14,569 player-day pairs with complete current-day wellness information and observed readiness on the following calendar day. Predictors measured no later than day $t$ were used to forecast readiness at $t+1$. A ridge autoregressive model using current readiness (\Pzero) and a ridge full-monitoring model using current readiness, daily and 7-day load, wellness, calendar, and reporting-density variables (\Pone) were each updated by the same sequential player-specific residual adjustment based on up to 28 earlier eligible forecast dates. Models were trained with 2020 data and tested in 2021 within each team and bidirectionally across teams. The primary estimand was \DeltaMAE{} = MAE(\Pone) $-$ MAE(\Pzero), with 95\% player-cluster bootstrap confidence intervals.

\textbf{Results:} The full monitoring model did not reduce mean absolute error in any evaluation setting. \DeltaMAE{} was +0.016 (95\% CI $-$0.001 to +0.036) within Team A, +0.056 (+0.009 to +0.102) within Team B, +0.047 (+0.005 to +0.099) from Team A to Team B, and +0.025 (+0.007 to +0.043) from Team B to Team A. Positive values indicate higher error for \Pone. Findings remained directionally consistent when the residual-history window was changed and when a reduced monitoring panel was evaluated.

\textbf{Conclusions:} In these two elite women's football teams, the evaluated daily workload--wellness panel did not demonstrate stable incremental forecasting value beyond a personalised autoregressive readiness baseline. This finding does not imply that workload or wellness are causally irrelevant; rather, it challenges the assumption that adding routine daily variables necessarily improves next-day self-reported readiness forecasts.
\end{abstract}

\textbf{Keywords:} athlete monitoring; women's football; readiness to train; training load; wellness; personalised forecasting; transportability
