\section{Methods}
\subsection{Study design and data source}
This was a secondary longitudinal forecasting analysis of the public SoccerMon dataset \citep{Midoglu2024,SoccerMonZenodo}. SoccerMon contains monitoring reports from two teams in the Norwegian women's elite football league during the 2020 and 2021 seasons. Training-load reports were completed after training or activity sessions, whereas wellness reports were intended to be completed once per day. The original data collection used the PmSys athlete-monitoring system, was based on written participant consent for open publication, and was approved by the Norwegian Privacy Data Protection Authority (reference 296155) \citep{Midoglu2024}. The present analysis used de-identified public data and did not involve participant contact.

\subsection{Prediction time point, analytic unit, target, and eligibility}
The analytic unit was a player-day pair. For player $i$ on calendar day $t$, all candidate predictors available by the end of day $t$ were used to forecast readiness at calendar day $t+1$ (Figure~\ref{fig:design}). This operational definition permits current-day wellness and same-day session-RPE to inform the forecast. The target was the next-calendar-day readiness-to-train score on a 1--10 self-reported scale; it was aligned to the next calendar date for the same player, rather than to the next available observation. The public files provide calendar dates but not complete within-day timestamps. The protocol is therefore a calendar-day forecasting design, not a fully timestamp-verified real-time deployment simulation.

The primary cohort was a paired complete-case comparison set. It retained player-days with a complete current-day readiness and wellness profile, an observed next-day readiness outcome, and the available 7-day load history used by the full model. The cohort contained 14,582 eligible pairs from 50 pseudonymised players. \Pzero{} itself does not require wellness information; the common complete-case cohort was used so that \Pzero{} and \Pone{} were compared on exactly the same player-days. The eligibility flow and team/year-specific missingness are reported in Figure~\ref{fig:flow} and Supplementary Tables~S4--S5. The primary estimand therefore applies to report-complete, outcome-observed monitoring days rather than to all calendar days.

\subsection{Candidate predictors and model definitions}
The primary comparator, \Pzero, used current-day readiness alone in its population prediction model. The full monitoring model, \Pone, used current-day readiness; daily load; load averaged over the 7-day interval ending on day $t$ (day $t$ plus the preceding six calendar days); the number of training days in the same interval; current fatigue, mood, sleep duration, sleep quality, soreness, and stress; numeric weekday (0--6) and month (1--12) covariates; and recent 3- and 7-day readiness-reporting rates. Daily load was session-RPE aggregated to the player-day level. Daily load and 7-day mean load were transformed as $\log(1+x)$ before modelling; no other candidate predictor was log-transformed. We did not simultaneously include a large set of derived workload indices, such as acute:chronic ratios, monotony, and strain, because they are mathematically related to the same duration and RPE inputs and may be highly collinear in SoccerMon \citep{Midoglu2024,Rago2020}. A reduced-monitoring model, \Ptwo, was examined in sensitivity analyses and included current readiness, daily load, fatigue, soreness, sleep duration, sleep quality, and the same calendar covariates.

\subsection{Population models, chronological tuning, and sequential personalisation}
For each evaluation scenario, linear/ridge population models were fitted only with the designated 2020 source-team data. Each comparator was tuned separately using three expanding chronological validation folds within the source team's 2020 data. Validation blocks began at approximately 55\%, 70\%, and 85\% of that team's unique observed 2020 dates. Candidate penalties were $\alpha=0,0.0001,0.001,0.01,0.1,1,10,100,1000,10000,100000$, where $\alpha=0$ denotes the unpenalised ordinary least-squares limit.

Tuning evaluated the complete deployed pipeline. For each candidate $\alpha$, a population prediction was generated for a validation block and then updated with the same past-only sequential residual procedure used in the 2021 evaluations. Residual history was reset at the start of every internal validation block to emulate a target-period cold start. The selected penalty minimised validation-size-weighted MAE of the fully adapted validation predictions; no 2021 target outcome was used for tuning (Supplementary Table~S1). This procedure tunes the deployed comparison rather than only the unadapted population component.

For each model fit, median imputation and standardisation were estimated from the corresponding source training partition only and then applied to validation or test data. Weekday was encoded numerically from 0 (Monday) to 6 (Sunday), and month from 1 to 12. These parsimonious linear encodings were retained for reproducibility; they do not fully represent potentially nonlinear or cyclical calendar effects and are considered in the limitations. Predictions were retained as continuous values and were neither rounded nor truncated to the 1--10 score range.

Let $\widehat{y}^{b(m)}_{i,t+1}$ denote the population prediction from model $m$ for player $i$ at time $t+1$. Both \Pzero{} and \Pone{} then received the same online player-specific residual adjustment:
\begin{equation}
\widehat{y}^{(m)}_{i,t+1} = \widehat{y}^{b(m)}_{i,t+1} + \frac{1}{|\mathcal{H}_{i,t}|}\sum_{s \in \mathcal{H}_{i,t}}\left(y_{i,s+1} - \widehat{y}^{b(m)}_{i,s+1}\right),
\end{equation}
where $\mathcal{H}_{i,t}$ contained up to the 28 earlier eligible forecast dates for the same player in the target 2021 period. At cold start, when no earlier target-period residual was available, the adjustment was zero. This protocol represents an online monitoring context in which earlier observed readiness outcomes are available but future outcomes are not. It is a light-touch updating strategy, conceptually related to prediction-model updating in a new setting, rather than a claim that a universal updating method is optimal \citep{Janssen2008}. The adaptation rule was deliberately identical for \Pzero{} and \Pone{} so that the primary comparison isolates the incremental contribution of the added monitoring panel beyond the same personal-history information.

\subsection{Evaluation settings}
Four leakage-controlled settings were evaluated: (1) Team A 2020 for training and Team A 2021 for testing; (2) Team B 2020 for training and Team B 2021 for testing; (3) Team A 2020 for training and Team B 2021 for testing; and (4) Team B 2020 for training and Team A 2021 for testing. The cross-team analyses are a two-team transportability assessment under sequential player-specific adaptation. Population-model parameters are transferred across teams, but each player's earlier observed target-team readiness outcomes subsequently inform residual updating. The cross-team analyses are therefore not zero-shot external validation. No random player-day split was used because the intended task was prospective forecasting with clustered repeated observations \citep{Bergmeir2018,Roberts2017,Debray2023TRIPODCluster}.

\subsection{Outcomes and statistical analysis}
The primary performance measure was mean absolute error (MAE), selected because it is interpretable in readiness-score points and is less dominated by occasional large deviations than squared-error measures. The primary estimand was $\Delta\mathrm{MAE}=\mathrm{MAE}(\Pone)-\mathrm{MAE}(\Pzero)$, so positive values favour the readiness-history baseline. Root mean squared error, mean bias, $R^2$, the percentage of predictions within $\pm1$ readiness point, and the intercept and slope from linear regressions of observed on predicted readiness were secondary diagnostics; they were not used to determine the primary conclusion.

We used 2,000 percentile player-cluster bootstrap resamples to construct 95\% confidence intervals for $\Delta\mathrm{MAE}$. Player clusters were resampled with replacement, and their total absolute-error contributions were pooled so that repeated player-days were not treated as independent observations. These intervals quantify uncertainty across players within the evaluated target team; they do not represent sampling uncertainty across a population of teams. No smallest practically important MAE difference, non-inferiority margin, or decision-utility threshold was prespecified. The analyses therefore compare predictive error and its uncertainty rather than establish formal equivalence, non-inferiority, clinical utility, or cost-effectiveness.

Sensitivity analyses varied the residual-history window (14, 28, and 56 prior forecast dates), evaluated \Ptwo, and fitted a wellness-item-missingness sensitivity model using source-training median imputation plus wellness-specific missingness indicators. This latter analysis was conditional on observed current- and next-day readiness and available 7-day load history; it addresses item-level wellness missingness within otherwise evaluable player-days, not days on which the daily readiness questionnaire was not completed. Analyses were performed in Python using pandas, scikit-learn, NumPy, and Matplotlib. The full workflow, environment specification, and validation checks are archived in the versioned reproducibility package cited in the Code availability statement.
